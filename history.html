The History of Mithila

From the ancient kingdom of Videha and the legendary King Janaka to the vibrant cultural renaissance of today — the history of Mithila is a story of resilience, faith, art, and timeless wisdom.

Mithila's history stretches back over 2,500 years, woven into the fabric of Indian civilization. It is a land of kings, sages, poets, and artists — a land that has inspired generations.

🏛️ The Kingdom of Videha ~ 600 BCE

The ancient kingdom of Videha, also known as Mithila, was one of the most prominent kingdoms of the Indian subcontinent. It was ruled by the legendary King Janaka, a great philosopher-king who is revered in Hindu mythology as the father of Goddess Sita.

The kingdom was known for its rich intellectual and spiritual traditions. The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, one of the oldest philosophical texts, is believed to have been composed in the courts of King Janaka. The kingdom also played a central role in the Ramayana, as it was the land where Lord Rama married Sita.

📜 Medieval Mithila ~ 800 – 1700 CE

During the medieval period, Mithila emerged as a major centre of learning and culture. The Maithili language flourished, and poets like Vidyapati (c. 1350–1450) composed lyrical poetry that is still celebrated today.

The region came under the rule of various dynasties, including the Karnats, the Oinwars, and later the Mughals. Despite political changes, Mithila maintained its distinct cultural identity, with its own traditions of art, music, and scholarship.

The Madhubani art tradition, which originated in this era, became a unique form of visual storytelling, with women painting intricate designs on walls and floors during festivals and ceremonies.

🕊️ Colonial & Modern Era ~ 1850 – Present

Under British colonial rule, Mithila witnessed significant social and educational reforms. The region produced many freedom fighters, scholars, and reformers who contributed to India's independence movement.

After India's independence in 1947, Mithila was divided between India (Bihar) and Nepal (eastern Terai). Despite the political division, the cultural and linguistic unity of Mithila remained strong.

In recent decades, Mithila has experienced a cultural renaissance. Maithili was recognized as one of the 22 official languages of India in 2003. Madhubani art has gained international acclaim, and the region's temples and festivals continue to attract pilgrims and tourists from around the world.
